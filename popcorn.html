Popcorn Web App

Overview

Popcorn, previously Lunchable, is an early-stage social-networking startup that helps college students make friends online. Throughout my time at Popcorn, I was directly involved in shaping the entire product development cycle of a web app that sets users up on group meetups with people of similar interests.

Role & Duration

Role: UX Designer

Team: 1 CEO/Project Manager, 5 Engineers, 2 Designers

Timeline: May 2020 – July 2020

Methods: Prototyping (Figma), User Flows (Miro), User Interviews, Usability Testing, Wireframing, Sketching

Final Product

Sign Up Process

Providing a standard, intuitive process for the user to register through.

Profile Creation

Helping users create engaging, personalized profiles through an enjoyable form filling process.

Scheduling a Meetup

Letting users schedule meetups efficiently while ensuring good matches.

Meeting Matches

Introducing users to their group matches in a personable way and actively encouraging them to attend the meetup.

Chatting

Giving users the opportunity to engage with current and past meetup groups.

Video Call

Providing a fun meetup room that fosters engaging conversation.

User Research

Receiving Initial Requirements
The internal stakeholder, my manager, strongly emphasized that the main value proposition of the product was the fact that it sets up a time and (virtual) place for users to meet the people they match with, removing the friction of coordinating a meetup themselves.
Conducting User Interviews
In order to confirm the requirements given to me and discover new user needs, I decided to conduct a round of user research. I interviewed 5 college students, ranging from freshmen to seniors. My goal for the study was to learn more about their experiences and preferences with finding friends in college, especially now that we’re all online.
Synthesizing Interview Insights
  • 80% of interviewees said they feel awkward when meeting up with someone they met online.
  • 40% of interviewees think existing friend-matching apps are unreliable in that they don’t guarantee a user to actually meet up, virtually or in person, with their matches.
  • 60% of interviewees said they find it difficult to start conversations with someone new due to not knowing what to talk about.
Refining the Requirements
My research confirmed the value of setting up meetups for the user. I also noted that a big problem not addressed by the initial requirements was that users feel awkward when starting conversations with new people. Thus, I proposed that we address this problem to my manager and arrived at the following problem statement.

The Problem

College students want to make new friends online, however they feel anxious about scheduling meet ups and potentially having awkward conversations.

The Solution

Popcorn aims to lift the stress that comes with meeting new people by facilitating high-participation in group meetups, building a sense of commonality early-on, and removing barriers in starting conversations.

Goal

Facilitate high turnout at meetups

Approaches

Organize all meetup logistics for users. Direct users to confirm that they’re participating early on.

Goal

Build a strong sense of group commonality

Approaches

Put together groups based on hobbies and interests and tell them what they have in common.

Goal

Remove barriers to starting conversation

Approaches

Provide conversation starters. Make chatting and reaching out as convenient as possible.

Concept Development

Mapping Out the Flow
Before I started prototyping, I mapped out the task flow. I focused on ordering the actions in a way that is intuitive, yet it still guides the user enough to alter their behavior, namely increasing their likelihood to sign up and show up to a meetup.
Finding the Right Approach
After deciding the flow, I roughly sketched out what each feature would look like in order to explore how to approach them and organize them into pages that made sense to the user.

Explored how to display an Upcoming Meetup, focusing on what information to display for each meetup.

Drew out a basic form to plan out the ordering and type of questions that would best engage the user.

Planned out how to allow meetup matches to start conversing early on.

Prototyping

Influencing Users to Attend the Meetup
The “Upcoming Meetup” page is extremely important in that it has the biggest impact on user behavior, namely influencing them to attend the meetup.

Iteration 1

Gives the user a small preview of their matches on the first page, with the option to view more information.

Iteration 2

Also gives the user a small preview of their matches on the first page, with a second page that emphasizes the group chat feature.

Iteration 3

Allows the user to see all information on their matches and meetup in one page. Introduces matches to the user in a friendly, personal way by providing personalized intros and attendance status right off the bat.

I decided to go with Iteration 3 because it allows users to get to know the other people at first glance, making their first impression more personable. Overall, this presentation of information actively encourages the user to learn about and communicate with their matches.

Usability Testing

Providing an Enjoyable Video Call Experience
Given that 80% interviewees mentioned how much they dread awkward conversations during first meetings, I wanted to incorporate some kind of conversation starter into the video call. I created mid-fidelity prototypes and conducted usability-testing with 6 different college students. During this first round of usability testing, 4 out of 6 users mentioned that they would love this feature, further confirming that it is a valuable feature.

Iteration 1

The user can refer to information about the other people while talking. 👎 4 out of 6 users said the sidebar was distracting and wished it was collapsable.

Iteration 2

Puts the conversation starters in the middle, in the form of cards, gamifying the conversation. 👎 All the users took a while to explore and figure out what actions were possible.

Iteration 3

All the action buttons are centralized at the bottom. 👍 Users easily discovered the possible actions and liked how the buttons didn’t block their view of the other people.

I ended up choosing Iteration 3 because compared to the others, it best allows users to put their attention on the people they’re talking to, while still giving them easy, non-obstructive access to conversation assistance if they need it.

Combining Stakeholders' Feedback

Fostering Meetup Participation
I asked users to go through the entire flow, giving them specific tasks along the way to observe their behavior. In addition to getting feedback from users, I also received feedback from my manager, and combined the feedback from both stakeholders to make the following changes:

  • Moved the “Confirm Now” button to a more visible and prominent position. This would encourage the user to click this button, thus furthering our number one goal of increasing meetup participation.

  • Changed the green checkmarks to text because users were confused about their meaning during usability testing. Now, users can clearly see which people are confirmed to attend the meetup, which would in turn encourage them to confirm as well.

  • Made the chat notification red in order to make it more visible, thus encouraging users to click on it and chat with their matches.

Reimagining How Users Connect with Past Meetups
I initially designed this page to show a history of the user’s past meetups, with each meetup represented with a card. After receiving feedback, I redesigned it with the following changes:

  • Changed “Past Meetups” page into a “Chats” page replacing the meetup cards with chats. This would make it more accessible and effortless for users to engage with past meetup groups, thus furthering our goal of fostering engagement.

  • Removed the chatbox from the “Upcoming Meetup” page and instead put all chats in together in one page in order to create a more centralized place for users to talk to others. During testing, users also confirmed that this format makes them feel more inclined to participate in the group chats.

Post-Project Reflection

Results
  • Handed off the final prototype to engineers, who have finished developing 80% of the app.
  • Achieved an average ease-of-use rating of 4.7 out of 5 for all the features in the final usability test.
Takeaways
  • It is important to quantify usability testing results, to help with making unbiased, logical changes to the design.
  • Product design is a balance between meeting business goals and user needs, and a designer must make tradeoff decisions based on the circumstance.
  • When working as a UX designer, it is important to learn how to justify design decisions in a business-focused standpoint in order to successfully propose design solutions in a company setting.
  • Consistent communication with team members is vital when building a product, especially in order to ensure consistent design.
What's Next
  • Delve into improving the interaction design of the web app, to make the user experience more enjoyable.
  • Explore features that fosters a larger community, to add more social value to the web app.
  • Identify more use cases beyond college students to expand the app’s user base.
